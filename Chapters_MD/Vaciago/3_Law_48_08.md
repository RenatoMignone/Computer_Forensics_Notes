## Context and Scope (Preliminary)

**Law 48/2008** is Italy's statute ratifying the **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime** (Council of Europe Convention on Cybercrime, Budapest, 2001) and incorporating its provisions into Italian domestic law. It is one of the most significant pieces of Italian cybercrime legislation and directly governs how digital evidence must be collected, preserved, and admitted in Italian criminal proceedings.

Key areas expected to be covered in this chapter:

- **Ratification provisions**: How Italy incorporated the Budapest Convention's substantive offences (Articles 2–10) into the Italian Penal Code and related statutes
- **Procedural amendments**: Modifications to the Italian Code of Criminal Procedure (*Codice di Procedura Penale*) governing:
  - Computer searches and seizures
  - The requirement to make a **forensic copy** rather than seizing original hardware wherever possible
  - Chain of custody requirements *as codified in Italian law*
- **The "double original"**: The Italian rule requiring that digital evidence be preserved in its original form while a certified copy is used for examination
- **Admissibility requirements**: What the Italian courts require for digitally acquired evidence to be admitted and relied upon
- **Key offences introduced or amended**:
  - Unauthorised access to a computer system (*accesso abusivo a sistema informatico* — Art. 615 ter c.p.)
  - Illegal interception of computer communications (*Art. 617 quater c.p.*)
  - Computer fraud (*frode informatica* — Art. 640 ter c.p.)
  - Data interference (*Art. 635 bis/ter/quater c.p.*)
- **Interaction with GDPR**: How evidence-gathering obligations under the criminal procedure code interact with data protection constraints introduced after Law 48/2008
