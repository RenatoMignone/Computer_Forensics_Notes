## 1. Overview and Innovations

**Law 48/2008** (ratified March 18, 2008) is the statute that formally integrated the **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime** into the Italian legal system. It represents a critical bridge between international principles and domestic criminal procedure.

The law introduced three foundational innovations:
1.  **International Harmonization:** Aligning Italian law with the other 68+ signatory states of the Budapest Convention to facilitate cross-border investigations.
2.  **Reorganization of Cybercrime Offenses:** Redefining traditional crimes (like damage) for the digital age and establishing the concept of "digital domicile."
3.  **Extension of Corporate Liability (Decree 231/2001):** Holding legal entities (companies) criminally responsible for IT crimes committed in their interest or benefit.

---

## 2. Reorganization of IT Under Italian Law

### 2.1 Illegal Access to an IT System (Art. 615-ter)
The core legal principle introduced is the **Search and Seizure of the "Digital Domicile."**
- **The Domicile Principle:** A computer system is equated to a private home. The crime of "Illegal Access" is triggered by violating the owner's *intent to exclude* others (*ius excludendi alios*).
- **Security Irrelevance:** It does not matter if the system is poorly secured (e.g., no password or "1234"). If the owner's intent is to keep the system private, unauthorized entry is a crime.
- **Inside Threats:** An employee who has valid credentials but uses them for purposes **outside their professional mandate** (e.g., extracting data to take to a competitor) commits illegal access.

> 📎 *Slide reference: "Illegal Access to an IT System" - Law 48/2008 establishes the same protections for digital spaces as for physical ones.*

### 2.2 Damage to IT Systems and Data (Art. 635-bis to quinquies)
Law 48/2008 updated the definition of "damage" to include non-physical, temporary disruptions:
- **Temporary Unavailability:** A **DDoS attack** or **Ransomware** that blocks access for an hour or a week is a crime, even if the data remains intact.
- **Unauthorized Deletion:** An employee deleting their logs or personal data from a company device against explicit policy is liable for "Damage to Data."

### 2.3 Falsehood and Integrity
- **Decriminalization (2016):** Altering private digital documents (e.g., changing text in an email) is no longer a standalone criminal offense unless it constitutes **Fraud** or involves **Public Documents**.
- **Deepfakes (2025 Prediction):** Legislation is evolving to address AI-generated falsehoods specifically.

## 3. Corporate Criminal Liability (Legislative Decree 231/2001)

Before 2008, companies in Italy could not be prosecuted for crimes (*Societas delinquere non potes*). Law 48/2008 changed this by adding IT crimes to the list of "predicate offenses" under **Decree 231/2001**.

### 3.1 Interest and Benefit
A company is liable if a cybercrime is committed by its employees or managers for the **interest** (the goal) or **benefit** (the actual gain) of the corporation.
- **Example:** An employee steals a competitor's database to help their company win a contract. Both the employee and the company are liable.

### 3.2 Sanctions: The "Death" of the Company
The consequences for the company can be devastating:
1.  **Pecuniary Fines:** Up to €1.5 Million.
2.  **Interdictory Sanctions:** Suspending the company's right to operate or participate in public tenders. Even a 2-month suspension can destroy a business's revenue and ability to pay salaries.

### 3.3 The Compliance Defense (Modello 231)
A company can avoid liability if it proves it implemented a robust **Compliance Program**:
- **IT Policies:** Clear rules on what employees can and cannot do.
- **Data Classification:** Strict controls on sensitive information.
- **Monitoring:** Active systems to detect and prevent unauthorized exfiltration.

## 4. Procedural Changes: Digital Evidence Handling

Law 48/2008 modified the **Italian Code of Criminal Procedure (CCP)** to provide a legal framework for digital forensics:

### 4.1 Specialized Judicial Units
Due to technical complexity, cybercrime investigations are centralized in **District Court of Appeal** units (e.g., Turin, Milan, Rome). Specialized Postal Police units handle the acquisition.

### 4.2 Search and Seizure (Art. 247 CCP)
- **Computer Searches:** Police must use "technical measures" to avoid altering original data.
- **Service Providers:** Authorities can seize data directly from servers in Data Centers (Aruba, Google Italy, etc.).
- **Forensic Copies:** The standard requirement is to create a **verified bitstream copy** (using hashing) rather than seizing the physical machine, allowing the business to continue operating.

### 4.3 Preservation Orders
Investigators can issue orders to providers to **preserve data for 90 days** (expandable to 6 months) while a formal seizure warrant or international MLAT request is processed.
