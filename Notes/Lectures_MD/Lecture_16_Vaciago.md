## 1. Professional Ethics and Tool Verification

Paolo Dal Checco introduced the concept of **forensic curiosity** through an "April Fool's" case study:
- **The Experiment:** He published a fake tool claiming to "convert hashes" (e.g., MD5 to SHA-256) without the original file. 
- **The Lesson:** Many professionals believed it because of his reputation (Authority Bias). In forensics, you must **test everything**. It is mathematically impossible to derive a high-entropy hash from a lower-entropy one without the source data.
- **Verification:** Always verify results obtained from black-box or closed-source tools using open-source alternatives or manual checks.

## 2. Advanced Investigative Techniques (Garlasco Case)

The Garlasco murder case is a landmark for Italian digital forensics due to both excellent and extremely poor practices.

### 2.1 Virtual Machines for Hypothesis Testing
Contemporary experts in the case did not just read report outputs. They created **Virtual Machines (VMs)** that mimicked the suspect's original hardware.
- **Benefit:** This allows the investigator to "be" the suspect—running the same software versions and seeing exactly how the OS reacts to user input. It proves whether a certain trace *could* have been generated by a specific action.

### 2.2 Super Timelines (Plaso)
- **Timeline:** Basic list of file creation/modification/access (MAC times) from the file system.
- **Super Timeline:** Aggregates data from the file system, OS journals, application logs, browser history, and file internal metadata into a single temporal graph.
- **Case Impact:** In high-profile cases, a super timeline allows for millisecond-level reconstruction of activity, which can break or support a "digital alibi."

## 3. The "Digital Alibi" and Technical Failures

The Garlasco case highlighted critical procedural errors that "tampered" with the truth:
- **Lack of Write Blockers:** The local Carabinieri (not trained in forensics) accessed the suspect's laptop repeatedly over 15 days without hardware write blockers.
- **Metadata Destruction:** They opened the suspect's thesis document and **saved it**, overwriting the very timestamps (Last Modified/Accessed) that could have proved he was working during the time of the murder.
- **Inadequate Custody:** Undocumented USB devices were connected to the evidence machine, and the suspect's phone was not acquired for 22 months.
- **Legal Classification Error:** Treating the laptop as a "collection of useful elements" (Art. 348) instead of a "technical examination" (Art. 359/360) allowed for non-forensic handling that would be illegal today under Law 48/2008.

## 4. The Career Path of a Forensic Expert

- **Certifications:** ISO 27001/27037 are useful for compliance but do not replace field experience.
- **Skills over Degrees:** Curiosity, GitHub contributions (writing your own tools/parsers), and participation in technical communities (Telegram/LinkedIn groups) are more valued by laboratories than a PhD alone.
- **Law Enforcement vs. Private Sector:** Public sector careers are stable and usefully serve the community, but involve a long "apprenticeship" before handling high-level digital evidence.

## Key Concepts & Definitions
| Term | Definition |
|------|------------|
| **Super Timeline** | A forensic technique involving the aggregation of all possible temporal artifacts into a single, unified chronological view. |
| **Digital Alibi** | Evidence from digital devices used to prove a suspect's presence or activity elsewhere at a specific time. |
| **348 vs 359/360** | Italian legal codes; 348 refers to general police activity, while 359/360 mandates high-standard technical expertise. |
| **PRNU** | Photo Response Non-Uniformity; a way to uniquely "fingerprint" a digital camera sensor based on its unique noise pattern. |

## Summary
- Forensic experts must maintain a "zero-trust" attitude toward tools and authority, verifying all results through testing.
- Virtual Machines are the gold standard for testing investigative hypotheses on specific hardware configurations.
- Metadata is fragile; the Garlasco case demonstrated how simple acts (opening and saving a file) by untrained officers can destroy a digital alibi.
- A "Super Timeline" is superior to a standard timeline as it includes application-level events and journal logs.
- The distinction between a general police search and a specialized technical forensic examination is legally critical for the admissibility of evidence.
- Professional forensics requires a mix of technical skill (coding parsers), legal awareness (admissibility), and behavioral psychology (correlating real-life events with digital traces).
